# Content Principles (the what and the mix)

**Core belief:** social media is about people, not a place to show off products. To grab
attention and build a brand you must understand your audience's needs. So create content
with a clear goal. These are the principles of WHAT to make and how to balance it. For the
month-by-month workflow that executes them, see content-planning; for video specifics, see
reels-video.

**Language:** produce output in the brand's language (default Slovak), in a plain, direct,
no-bullshit voice.

## 1. Make content about your audience, not your brand
One of the biggest mistakes is communicating only about yourself - product photos, posts
about the company. That appeals to us marketers; the audience doesn't care.

Research what the audience actually cares about. You can do this right here with an AI
assistant - paste this starter prompt and fill the brackets:

```
Som [dopln kto si], ake problemy riesia moji potencialni zakaznici najcastejsie na
internete? Prosim, neber do uvahy len problemy ludi, ktori aktualne hladaju [dopln kto si],
ale vsetkych, ktori by raz mohli potrebovat [dopln kto si]. Co vyhladavaju na internete?
```

**Exercise (set aside ~1 hour) - plan a full month of content:**
1. Write down the problems and worries of the people you want to reach (the ones the AI
   surfaced).
2. Create posts that help them with those problems - first versions are enough (a rough,
   "ugly" written-out idea).
3. Lay out at least **12-15 of these first versions** into the calendar for the next month.
4. Done. When you have them, do nothing more - just schedule a day and time to develop them
   into second versions or to shoot the videos / make the graphics.

**Pro-tip:** cover topics adjacent to your core. If you're an architect, also give advice on
mortgages - people then see you as someone who truly understands their needs, not just your
own niche.

## 2. Quick hack - the idea bank (napadovnik)
To use inspiration fully, always keep an idea bank handy - one place (paper, a notes app, an
online sheet) where you jot every idea, even ones that hit during morning coffee or a walk.
The mind is fickle; if you don't note an idea immediately, you'll most likely lose it.

## 3. Three content types - Hero, Hub, Hygiene
Every post should fit one of three categories. It can work differently per brand, but the
simple split is by PURPOSE:

**Hero - reach and awareness.** Meant to reach broadly and get the largest relevant reach, so
even people who don't know you discover you. It need not be about you at all. Example: a hotel
makes a short TikTok/Instagram video about a beautiful hidden spot in its wider surroundings.
Publish it when you want reach, to attract new people, or to ride current trends.
- Spreads brand awareness, attracts new followers and prospects, raises engagement (which
  lifts the reach of your other posts).

**Hub - relationship and authority.** Deepens the relationship with your existing audience.
Delivers valuable info, advice, tips, so people see you as an authority. Example: instead of
promoting rooms, the hotel posts a short vlog with its chef sharing tips on local specialties
from seasonal regional produce - showcasing skill, supporting the local community, building
trust. Publish it continuously to hold existing followers' attention.
- Builds trust and loyalty, strengthens your expert position, creates a long-term audience
  that comes back.

**Hygiene - direct selling.** Direct sales content: products, services, special offers,
campaigns. Example: the hotel presents a weekend package with a massage included, or newly
renovated rooms with an intro discount, with a call to book. Publish it sparingly, when you
want to motivate a purchase or booking directly.
- Converts followers into paying customers, reminds people what you offer and why it's worth it.

**The mix:** balance the three by a percentage split across your content. Lean toward
reach/relationship (Hero + Hub); keep direct selling (Hygiene) the smallest share - do not let
selling content dominate the feed.

> Note: "Hero/Hub/Hygiene" also has a video-production lens (by production effort/cost) in the
> reels-video skill. This skill holds the strategic purpose-based definition (reach /
> relationship / sell); they describe the same buckets from different angles.

## 4. Every post stands on its own
Social media is information chaos and algorithms often show a user just one of your posts. So
even that single post must have a clear goal and point, even if it belongs to a series. Ask:
- What is this post's goal? (entertain, educate, inspire, sell?)
- What do I want the follower to do after reading/watching it?

Give each post exactly ONE goal (e.g. get comments, shares, or raise awareness). Chase several
goals at once and the viewer gets lost and you achieve nothing properly.

## 5. Quantity over quality
In content marketing, testing and regular output matter most. Not every post must be perfect -
try, watch what works, adapt. Treat each post as a test; if it doesn't land, try another format
or topic. Don't fear that a weak post will damage your brand (brand damage happens other ways,
not through a normal post). A volume-first approach lets you:
- Get data on what the audience cares about and what works.
- Improve your content faster.
- Reduce the pressure of perfectionism.

Then recycle the posts you see working, for as long as they keep working.

## 6. Recycle content
If something grabbed attention once, it will probably work again. Recycling saves time and
sustains results.
- Find older posts that performed well (many shares, comments).
- Refresh or rework them - add new info, change the graphics, turn text into video.
- Reuse one idea across formats (blog -> carousel -> video).

## 7. Summary - how to apply this
1. Build a content plan on the Hero-Hub-Hygiene model.
2. Focus on regular publishing - quantity over perfectionism.
3. Every post must have a clear, single goal.
4. Track post numbers and recycle the best ones.
5. Adapt content to the audience's needs, not just your brand.

The goal of content is to grab attention, educate, and build trust. Conversions on social are
not luck; they come from strategies that work.
