# Creative Techniques Library

Great campaigns are not random. They use repeatable techniques - patterns that appear
across different categories, budgets, and decades. When you know the patterns, you apply
them deliberately instead of hoping inspiration arrives. AI generates concepts fast when
you give it a technique to work with.

**Language:** default output in Slovak, plain and direct.

---

## Part 1: Content and ad creative techniques

### 1. Intentional Defect
Show your product or service broken, wrong, or incomplete in a way that reveals a
deeper truth or benefit. Counter-intuitive. Stops the scroll because something is off.

**Example:** Burger King's "Moldy Whopper" - a 34-day timelapse of their burger
decomposing. Point: no preservatives. The opposite of how a food brand normally shows
its product. The broken product proved the benefit no clean food shot ever could.

**When to use:** when you have a genuine differentiator that sounds boring stated
directly. The "broken" version SHOWS it instead of SAYING it.

**AI prompt to apply it:**
```
Pouzij techniku "Zamerny defekt" na tento brief: [brief].
Aky je najsilnejsi benefit mojho produktu, ktory sa da ukazat cez jeho
nedokonalost alebo obmedzenie? Vygeneruj 5 konceptov.
```

---

### 2. Crisis Pivot
When something goes wrong - a shortage, a delay, a mistake, bad press - pivot
publicly and make it a brand moment instead of hiding it.

**Example:** Wingstop ran out of chicken wings. Instead of apologizing, they
"rebranded" to "Thighstop" overnight, covered everything in green tape, and sold only
thighs. Transparency plus nerve equals viral - they got more PR than any planned
campaign could have bought.

**When to use:** when a real problem hits and you have the nerve to lean into it
publicly. Only works with genuine commitment - a half-hearted version reads as
damage control, which is worse than staying quiet.

---

### 3. Comparison Stat
Lead with a specific number that reframes your category. Numbers create credibility
and stop scrolling faster than adjectives.

Patterns:
- "This approach outperformed [old way] by [X]%."
- "[X]% of [audience] have [problem] without knowing [simple solution]."
- "We cut [bad metric] from [A] to [B] by changing one thing."

**Warning:** verify numbers before using. A stat that turns out to be fake destroys
trust permanently and is not recoverable.

---

### 4. Curiosity Hook ("See What Happens")
Open with an action or outcome without explaining why. The viewer must keep watching
to get the answer.

Classic formats:
- "See what happens when you [unexpected action]."
- "I tried [unusual thing] for 30 days. Here's what happened."
- "Stop doing [X]. Do this instead." (no explanation of "this" until 5 seconds in)
- "Most [audience] get this completely wrong."

---

### 5. Honest Confession
"The honest truth about [X] that changed everything for me." Direct to camera,
vulnerability as the hook. Signals that what follows is real, not scripted.

Why it works: people are trained to spot performed authenticity. When someone leads
with a confession or admits a mistake, the guard drops. The content that follows gets
heard differently.

**High saves, high shares.** Best for educational or personal-brand content.

---

### 6. Tutorial Cut (Right Way / Wrong Way)
Rapid cuts: wrong approach → right approach. No introduction, no context setup.
Start inside the first "wrong" moment.

Formats:
- "Stop doing this (show mistake) → do this instead (show fix)."
- "3 mistakes [audience] makes → what to do instead."
- Quick visual demo: wrong setup → correct setup → result.

Attention principle: the "wrong" must be something the viewer actually does. If they
don't recognize themselves in the mistake, they skip.

## Part 2: Storytelling frameworks

### But/Therefore (the South Park method)
In any story or post, replace "and then" with "but" or "therefore":
- "And then" = boring sequence of events. No tension.
- "But" = conflict, obstacle, reversal. Forces the reader forward.
- "Therefore" = consequence, decision, movement. Shows causality.

**Flat version (and-then):**
"I started a business. And then I hired staff. And then we got clients. And then
we grew."

**Engaging version (but/therefore):**
"I started a business. But I hired the wrong people. Therefore I had to fire half
the team in month three. But that forced me to build a real hiring process. Therefore
we've never made that mistake again."

Same story. Completely different reader experience.

**Exercise:** read any draft and count how many "and thens" appear (including implied
ones). Replace each with "but" or "therefore". No other changes needed.

---

### Emotional Arc
Map your content to a recognizable emotional shape:

- **Fall then rise:** starts bad, gradually improves. Classic redemption. Used by
  every comeback story, every "I almost quit" post.
- **Rise then fall then rise:** starts well, hits a crisis, recovers stronger.
  Hero's journey in miniature. Works for case studies and founder stories.
- **Normal then disruption then resolution:** everyday life → unexpected problem →
  clever fix. Best for how-to content and product demos.

The arc applies even to short posts. A LinkedIn post about a lesson has an arc:
before (mistake) → turning point (realization) → after (new behavior).

---

### In medias res (start in the middle)
No intro. No "hi, my name is". Start inside the action. Let context arrive naturally.

**Slow start:**
"Hi everyone, today I want to talk about something that happened to me last month
that I think you'll find really interesting and relevant to your work..."

**In medias res:**
"Three hours before the pitch, the client called and said they were going with
someone else." [story continues from there]

Same story. The first loses 60% of readers in the opening line. The second keeps them.

## Part 3: Naming techniques

When naming a brand, product, campaign, or event - try all of these before settling:

| Technique | What it is | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Intentional misspelling** | Drop a letter, change a vowel | Flickr, Tumblr, Digg, Reddit |
| **Foreign word** | A word from another language that captures the concept without a local equivalent | concept, brand feel depends on language |
| **Category descriptor** | Name the new category, not the product feature | "Pipeline" (not "EasyCRM"), "Notion" (not "NoteOrganizer") |
| **Portmanteau** | Merge two relevant words | Pinterest (pin + interest), Instagram (instant + telegram) |

The test: say the name out loud to someone unfamiliar with it. If they can spell it
and remember it the next day, it works.

## Part 4: The 4C campaign strategy framework

Before creative work, find where your strategy has genuine traction. Four lenses:

| C | Question to answer | Why it matters |
|---|-------------------|----------------|
| **Culture** | What is happening in society right now that is relevant? | Ads that meet people where their mind already is require zero persuasion |
| **Category** | What tension or gap does your industry ignore? | The ignored truth in a category is often the most powerful creative territory |
| **Customer** | What does your customer actually want or fear? | Not what you assume - what they tell you in reviews, messages, and complaints |
| **Company** | What can only YOUR brand say credibly? | Prevents the strategy from sliding to generic territory anyone could claim |

The best creative brief lives where all four intersect. If an idea only touches one
C, it will feel either irrelevant, inside-baseball, or uncredible.

**AI application:**
```
Pomoz mi najst strategicku prilezitosc pre tento brief: [brief].
Analyzuj cez 4C ramec:
- Kultura: co sa teraz deje v spolocnosti, co je relevantne?
- Kategoria: aku tenziu alebo medzeru ignoruje toto odvetvie?
- Zakaznik: co skutocne chce alebo sa boji moja cielova skupina?
- Znacka: co moze povedat verovhodne iba moja znacka?
Navrhni, kde sa tieto styri pretinaju.
```

## Part 5: Using AI to apply techniques

**The pattern:**
1. Choose the technique that fits the brief and the medium.
2. Give AI the technique name + brief + at least one constraint (30-second video,
   LinkedIn post, billboard, email subject line).
3. Ask for 5 concepts, not 1. Then pick and push the best one further.

**Choosing the right technique:**
```
Tu je moj brief: [brief]. Ktora z tychto kreatívnych technik by priniesla
najoriginalnejsi vysledok a preco? Techniky: Zamerny defekt, Krizovy pivot,
Porovnavacia statistika, Zvedavostny hook, Uprimne vyznanie, Tutorial cut.
Zdovodni svoju volbu pred tym, ako zacnes generovat.
```

Forcing the AI to reason before it generates significantly improves quality.

## Part 6: Learning from existing campaigns

AI can analyze video ads and extract their creative and strategic logic.

**Gemini can watch video.** Upload an MP4 or pass a YouTube link (via API). It describes
what is said, what is on screen, and can answer analytical questions about the content.

**Campaign analysis prompt:**
```
Pozri si toto video. Povedz mi:
1. Aku kreatívnu techniku pouziva a preco funguje?
2. Ako je strukturovanych prvych 5 sekund?
3. Aka je strategia za tym - kto je cielovka, co im hovoria, preco prave takto?
4. Co by som mohol aplikovat na svoju kategoriu?
```

**Building a campaign archive:**
Run this analysis on every great campaign you find. Save the outputs. After 50+
campaigns, you have a searchable creative reference database. Ask AI to find entries
by concept ("campaigns that use vulnerability", "campaigns that reframe a failure")
and it retrieves relevant ones by meaning, not just keywords.

## How to use this skill

1. Start with a technique, not a blank page. Pick one that fits the brief and medium.
2. Run the 4C check before creative work - it sharpens strategy so the creative
   territory is right before you generate executions.
3. Apply storytelling frameworks to any content format: post, video script, pitch,
   presentation.
4. When naming, test all 4 naming types before defaulting to a descriptor.
5. Build a campaign archive by running the Gemini analysis on great ads you find.
