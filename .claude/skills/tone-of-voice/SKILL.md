# Tone of Voice

**The premise:** ~95 million posts are added to Instagram every day, yet people can identify
a brand (Coca-Cola, McDonald's, Mastercard) from a single fictional post. The magic is a
correctly set tone of voice plus brand codes. Tone of voice is one of the core elements you
set for every brand.

**Two anchors that run through everything:** drive the voice from (1) the audience's
**problems** and (2) your **solutions** to those problems. And: marketing is a conversation,
never a monologue.

**Language:** produce output in the brand's language (default Slovak). Keep a plain, direct,
no-bullshit voice.

## The 4 exercises (run them in order)

### 1. Three principles of tone of voice (brand values)
Give the brand 3 values it will stand behind. Derive them from the audience's problem and
the solution. Find the *why* hidden behind the *what*.

Worked example: for a brand that teaches marketing, the *what* problem is that educators
explain things badly; the *why* behind it is **simplicity** - those educators are great
marketers, so their content can be too high-level for some. So the brand commits to simple
content anyone can understand, with analogies and "try it on yourself" tips. Its 3 (really 4)
principles become: simplicity, interaction, no-bullshit-ness, plus regularity.

**Exercise:** look at your brand canvas and find values close to the brand. Use a list of
values if helpful. Take your time.

### 2. Brand personality
Brands are like people. Personality is how the brand talks and behaves. People enjoy
conversing with brands they find likable.

Guiding question: if the brand stood up and left the room, what would people say about it?
What is it like?

**Exercise:** which famous person does the brand resemble? Write down 3 people and pick 3
traits you like about them. Is the person funny? Serious? Deep or surface-level? Do they use
informal address? Are they spontaneous? From the 9 traits (3 people x 3 traits), pick the
ones that fit your communication best. Constantly compare these traits against the brand's
values and canvas.

### 3. Target audience (make it your judge)
Imagine the brand as one person and the client (target audience) as another. They are not
the same person. Invent a personality for the client too: who are they?

Use a worksheet of ~27 questions to define the tone precisely. While answering, always
picture both the brand personality and the client. Use the client as the executioner: for
every answer ask "would the client be happy with this?" If not, the conversation with your
audience will not be easy - adjust to suit them.

Questions to settle here include: informal or formal address? Speaking to a man, a woman, an
individual, or a crowd?

### 4. Rules of the game (communication templates / brand codes)
Create your brand's own rules of the game - communication templates. They can be a document
or a visual canvas, whatever suits you (visual types use templates; writers can write the
rules out instead).

**Brand codes** are the set of elements you reuse in communication so people recognize you.
They can hit all 5 senses. Example: a brand can train its audience to expect, every morning
at a fixed time (when people hold their phone after waking up or arriving at work and
scroll), one loud, instantly recognizable post.

Examples of possible brand codes: photos turned upside down; a fixed loud palette (pink,
blue, yellow, black, white, red); a signature emoji set; no full stops at the end of lines in
slides; signature graphics. A public example: McDonald's codes are the golden arches, red,
the Big Mac, the clown, the letter M, the smell of fries on arrival.

**Set:** tonality, values, personality, and make sure the whole thing pleases the target
audience. Choose informal/formal address, choose colors and elements (codes) that can work on
all 5 senses, and stick to it.

## Notes to carry forward
- The rules of the game are living: they age and mature like wine. Update, change, and add to
  them meaningfully over time, but keep the essence the same.
- A brand is NOT "whatever the current brand manager says it is." Define it with the exercises
  above instead.

## How to use this skill
1. Run the 4 exercises in order; do not skip to brand codes before values/personality are set.
2. Always tie each choice back to the audience's problem and the solution.
3. Produce a concrete artifact: the 3 values, the personality (with reference person and
   traits), the address/audience decisions, and a list of brand codes plus 1-2 reusable post
   templates.
4. Sanity-check every decision with "would the target client be happy with this?"
